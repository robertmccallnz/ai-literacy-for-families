AI Literacy for Families

Why this exists

Most "AI literacy for families" material on the internet is written by, or paid for by, the companies that profit from kids using AI products. It tends to focus on personal responsibility — teach your child to be careful, supervise their screen time, set good boundaries — while treating the business model behind the technology as a fixed feature of the world. That framing is not neutral. It quietly transfers the cost of corporate decisions onto individual households, and it leaves the harder structural questions out of the conversation entirely.

This course tries to do something different. It still gives families practical tools — discussion prompts, activities, fact-checking habits, a household agreement — because parents and teens have to live in the world as it actually is. But it situates those tools inside an honest socio-economic frame:

  • AI is not a neutral consumer product. It is built, owned, and optimised by a small number of very large corporations, and the design choices that shape your child's experience were made in service of those corporations' interests — not your family's.
  • The "free" tools are not free. When a 14-year-old pours their anxieties into an AI companion, that conversation is a labour input to a commercial product. The political economy of that exchange matters, and teens deserve to understand it.
  • "Bias" is not a bug to be patched. It is the predictable output of training a model on the unequal world we already have. Discussions of AI bias that stop at "the dataset needs to be more diverse" miss the structures that produced the unequal dataset in the first place.
  • Privacy is a class issue. Wealthy families can afford the time, expertise, and paid tools to insulate themselves from surveillance. Working-class and migrant families largely cannot. A real conversation about "digital boundaries" has to acknowledge that the rules are not the same for everyone.
  • Critical thinking is a collective skill, not a personal virtue. The SIFT method works. So does talking to people who know things. So does trusting your union, your local librarian, your community elders, and your political organisation over a chatbot owned by a hedge fund.
  • The most powerful "household digital boundary" is collective action. Strong privacy laws, regulated advertising, ed-tech procurement standards, and worker rights inside AI companies will protect your kid more than any opt-out toggle. Teach that alongside the toggles.

The course doesn't lecture on any of this. It asks questions — together, between a parent and a teen — that lead families to notice the structures for themselves. The political reading is the scaffolding; the conversations are the building.

Who this is for

  • Parents and guardians of kids aged roughly 12–18 who want to understand AI well enough to talk about it honestly at home.
  • Teens who'd rather think critically about the tech than be lectured about it.
  • Teachers, librarians, youth workers, union educators, community organisers who want a ready-made curriculum to run with the families they serve.
  • Translators and adapters — please localise this for your own language, country, and community context. The structure is intentionally portable.

You do not need a technical background. You do not need to be confident about AI yourself. The course is built around the assumption that the adult and the teen are learning together.

The 6-week course

A self-contained curriculum covering:

  1. How AI actually works — pattern-matching, not magic
  2. Bias & deepfakes — structural problems with the same root
  3. Privacy & ethics — who owns the conversation when you talk to a chatbot
  4. Critical thinking & fact-checking — the SIFT method
  5. Setting household digital boundaries — co-written, not imposed
  6. Modeling responsible tech habits — parents commit too

Each weekly module includes learning objectives, discussion prompts, an at-home activity to do as a parent–teen pair, a vetted resource list, and a closing reflection.

The companion workbook

A 13-page printable PDF with:

  • A Household AI Agreement template (five sections + signature fields)
  • A SIFT Cheat Sheet including a cut-out 2-minute checklist
  • Mirror Commitment Cards for the Week 6 activity
  • A monthly check-in panel to keep the practice alive

The Substack pack

Eight ready-to-paste posts (intro + six weekly modules + wrap-up), rewritten in a more conversational long-form voice. Includes a short note on free-vs-paid publishing models if you want to run this as a paid newsletter series.

How to use it

As a family, at home

  1. Download the course PDF and the workbook PDF.
  2. Schedule one 60–90 minute session a week for six weeks.
  3. Read each module separately first, then meet to work through the discussion prompts and at-home activity together.
  4. In Week 5, fill out the Household AI Agreement together. Sign it. Put it on the fridge.
  5. In Week 6, fill out the Mirror Commitment Cards. Schedule the monthly check-in.

As a teacher, librarian, or community educator

  1. Run the course as a six-week cohort, one module per week.
  2. Print one workbook per family.
  3. Optional: schedule a 30-minute live debrief at the end of each week so parents and teens can share what came up.

As a newsletter publisher

  1. Open the Substack pack.
  2. Copy each post (separated by ---) into Substack's editor.
  3. Schedule one post per week, same day each week.
  4. Free intro and wrap-up; the six core weeks can be free or paid.

Building from source

The Markdown files are the source of truth. The PDF and DOCX in this repo were generated from them.

Markdown → PDF (simple):

pandoc course/ai_literacy_course.md -o course/ai_literacy_course.pdf

Markdown → PDF (the styled version in this repo): The styled PDFs were built with Python + ReportLab (course) and Python + WeasyPrint (workbook). If you want to rebuild them with the same styling, open an issue and I can share the build scripts.

Markdown → Word:

pandoc course/ai_literacy_course.md -o course/ai_literacy_course.docx

Markdown → HTML for a website:

pandoc course/ai_literacy_course.md -o index.html --standalone

A note on what this course doesn't do

  • It doesn't tell you which AI tools to allow or ban. That's a decision your family makes together, in your own context, with the agreement you draft in Week 5.
  • It doesn't promise to "AI-proof" your kid. Nothing can. The technology will change, the products will change, and your job is to give them habits that survive those changes — not a list of rules from 2026.
  • It doesn't pretend the structural problems can be solved by good parenting. They can't. But good parenting paired with collective political action is more effective than either on its own.
  • It doesn't sell anything. There are no affiliate links, no upsells, no paid tiers. Every linked resource is something you can access for free or through a public library.
